# Method: Inversion

## Purpose

Understand a goal, belief, or problem by reversing it.

This method reveals hidden assumptions, failure modes, and overlooked constraints.

## When to Use

- The user wants to improve a behavior, decision, plan, or understanding.
- Direct thinking is stuck.
- The system needs to expose what would make an outcome fail.
- The user is overfocused on success and ignoring risk.

## When NOT to Use

- The user needs encouragement or emotional steadiness first.
- The issue is already framed negatively and inversion would deepen rumination.
- The task is straightforward explanation or memorization.
- The reversed frame is too artificial to produce insight.

## Inputs

- Goal, belief, or desired outcome.
- Current plan or understanding.
- Known risks or constraints.
- Context for action or interpretation.

## Workflow

1. State the desired outcome.
2. Reverse the question: "How would this fail?" or "What would the opposite imply?"
3. Identify behaviors, assumptions, or conditions that produce the unwanted outcome.
4. Use the negative map to clarify what matters.
5. Convert insights into principles, checks, or small actions.

## Outputs

- Failure modes.
- Hidden assumptions.
- Preventive principles or checks.
- Revised plan or understanding.

## Success Criteria

- The reversed frame reveals something not visible directly.
- The user gains practical or conceptual clarity.
- The output reduces avoidable failure.
- The method does not become pessimistic rumination.

## Memory Integration

- Save recurring failure patterns to Learning Profile.
- Link inversion insights to Practice Design outputs.
- Record changed assumptions in Cognitive Change History.
- Add useful checks to future review prompts.

## Compatible Skills

- Deep Dive
- Application
- Reflection
- Synthesis
