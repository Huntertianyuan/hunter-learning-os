# Method: Synthesis

## Purpose

Combine multiple ideas, sources, domains, or perspectives into a higher-order understanding.

This method helps the learner build connections and generate insight beyond any single source.

## When to Use

- The user wants to connect books, disciplines, concepts, or experiences.
- Multiple ideas seem related but their relationship is unclear.
- The user is building a long-term theme or personal framework.
- A conversation has produced several partial insights that need integration.

## When NOT to Use

- The individual concepts are not yet understood.
- The user needs precise distinction before integration.
- The synthesis would force connections that are shallow or artificial.
- The task is memorization or direct application.

## Inputs

- Concepts or sources to connect.
- Existing Idea Network or prior learning records.
- User's current question or theme.
- Similarities, tensions, and differences among the ideas.

## Workflow

1. List the ideas or sources being connected.
2. Identify shared structures, themes, or problems.
3. Identify differences or tensions.
4. Form a higher-level model or insight.
5. Test the synthesis against at least one concrete example.
6. Name what is newly understood because of the connection.

## Outputs

- Cross-source connection.
- Higher-level model or insight.
- Tensions that remain unresolved.
- Updated idea network links.

## Success Criteria

- The synthesis produces new understanding, not just a list of similarities.
- Differences are preserved rather than flattened.
- The user can explain the connection in their own words.
- The synthesis can guide future reading, thinking, or action.

## Memory Integration

- Update Idea Network with cross-domain links.
- Save major integrations as Cognitive Change History candidates.
- Update Learning Profile with recurring themes.
- Preserve unresolved tensions as future inquiry anchors.

## Compatible Skills

- Synthesis
- Deep Dive
- Application
- Reflection
